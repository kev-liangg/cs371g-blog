Entry #3

  • February 7, 2021
  • Kevin Liang
What did you do this past week?

This past week I mostly had to sort out some non-academic related affairs aside from the regular homework in my other classes. I set up my environment for the Collatz project but I haven’t had the chance to delve into the work yet.

What's in your way?

I don’t think anything’s blocking my way. I’m familiar with Dr. Downing’s workflow as well as the expectations for the Collatz project and I’m confident I’ll make the time to submit everything by the deadline.

What will you do next week?

I will finish up Collatz for early next week starting ASAP and try to start my other homeworks earlier than usual. I’ve definitely been pushing most of my work closer to the deadlines. I’m usually good at gauging the time I need for each assignment and finishing it comfortably within time, but I should make it a better habit to get things out of the way earlier if I’m confident about how long it will take anyway.

If you read it, what did you think of the Continuous Integration?

I found it mostly interesting to see how the Continuous Integration paper written in 2006 stacks up with today’s modern technologies and toolchains. The best practices for software engineering are always changing, and the procedure we’re learning about in this course will definitely become outdated as the industry shifts and adapts. To me, having a more dated paper certainly emphasizes why it is important to understand the underlying reasons that drive the idea of Continuous Integration rather than focus on the specific usage of the technologies themselves.

What was your experience of Collatz?

I really like the Collatz project and think it’s perfect to start out this class. It applies a lot of what we’re learning in lecture and reading in Perusall in terms of software development best practices, and leaves a lot of independent inquiry into concepts like caching and optimizations.

What was your experience of exceptions?

I’m comfortable with exceptions as a concept, but of course the implementation is always language-specific. I’ve never worked with exceptions in Python or JavaScript before, so it took me a bit longer to grasp the usage patterns and specific behavior. I will probably have to rewatch the lecture again and walk through the steps before I feel fully confident about exceptions in those contexts.

What made you happy this week?

I scored a perfect 250 points out of 250 on my LTC qualification, which means I can officially start the licensing application soon. I know it’s not difficult to achieve that score, but I’m happy with my groupings despite being a bit out of practice with the current circumstances. There’s definitely room for improvement, but I’m otherwise confident in moving forward with things. My mother has always told me it’s a big responsibility to get licensed; it completely changes one’s habits and perspective. But when it comes to being accountable for my own safety, I know it will all be worth it.

What's your pick-of-the-week or tip-of-the-week?

As recruiting season starts back up, my tip-of-the-week is Huntr. This tool is essentially a one-stop shop to track job applications in a visually appealing and organized manner. I’ve definitely forgotten about several applications before and gotten spooked by an unexpected HackerRank email, so I’m hoping to use this tool in the future as I delve into the much scarier side of full-time job hunting.
