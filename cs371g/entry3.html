Entry #3

  • June 21, 2020
  • Kevin Liang
What did you do this past week?

This past week, I completed and submitted the Collatz project. I also annotated and read the assigned papers regarding Cultural Competence and Pair Programming. Most recently, I spent time familiarizing myself with the upcoming Netflix project, mostly by studying the file formats in preparation to build the necessary caches.

What's in your way?

The main thing in my way will probably be building the GitLab repository for the Netflix project from scratch since we are not provided a public repo this time. In the proper spirit of Pair Programming, I plan to set up the makefile and GitLab CI configuration alongside my partner in order to avoid mistakes and gain a shared understanding of the topics. The rest of the project seems relatively straightforward from the requirements, although my partner and I both predicted it will take more time than the previous due to the wider scope of the problem and optimizations.

What will you do next week?

I plan to continue working on the Netflix project with my partner, and hopefully run into issues sooner than later so we can get assistance during office hours. I also plan to begin studying for the first exam the following week. Although I am very confident in all of the material covered so far, I want to review everything separately in order to catch the more tricky and obscure cases.

What was your experience of exceptions, consts, and Boost serialization?

Exceptions were familiar to me from my Java experience. The behavior of the try/catch blocks seemed about the same, although the different types of catch statements took some time to fully understand. From my previous C++ experience as well, I understood the idea of const variables, const pointers, and const references. The use of const after a pointer was new to me, but it made sense after thinking of it as an immutable reference. Finally, const structs, functions, and expressions were new to me as well, but I was able to figure them all out quickly with some outside sources after the lecture.

I was able to comprehend the reasoning and advantages behind Boost serialization as presented in the lecture as well, but its actual usage didn't make sense to me. I plan to search for specific use cases to follow as examples when building the caches for the Netflix project, which should help me with my understanding there.

How are you doing and holding up? What's been most helpful for you in terms of support at this time?

I am doing better than last week. I have been making an effort to sleep earlier on the days before class so I can wake up earlier to study for the quizzes and eat breakfast. Over the week, food has been the most helpful in supporting me. I bought a large pack of spicy instant noodles for quick and easy meals in the mornings. I also started going out for drives and getting comfort food like Wendy's and Taco Bell, which is always a good break from the stress at home.

What made you happy this week?

I finally spent the money to purchase several pieces of gear and equipment for a competitive hobby of mine, which I am really excited about. The current situation has caused a significant delay in the shipping times, but I am still looking forward to the day when they finally arrive.

What's your pick-of-the-week or tip-of-the-week?

My pick-of-the-week is Eclipse's C++ debugger, as it has helped me identify and fix all of my bugs in the Collatz projects. If you've ever used the built-in Eclipse debugger for Java projects, the interface is essentially the same. My main system is Windows, so I had to install MinGW and use Eclipse's auto-generated build settings instead of the makefile.

Once I got the code running equivalently, the Eclipse debugger allowed me to easily set breakpoints, monitor variables, and track function calls all within a comfortable GUI. The official Eclipse documentation gives basic step-by-step instructions, though I found it more helpful watching Walter Schilling's video tutorial on the topic. I also found this article by Tracy Miranda about more advanced debugging features that I didn't know about before. It would have been really helpful to have known about them during the Collatz project, but at least they can be of use in the future now.
