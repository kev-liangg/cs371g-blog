Entry #2

  • June 14, 2020
  • Kevin Liang
What did you do this past week?

This last week, I completed the first Perusall submission on Continuous Integration. I made sure to put my full attention into reading the paper and writing my annotations, as I feel that the topic is very applicable to software development in any industry. Although I have a good understanding of the Collatz project, I did not begin the actual code until late this weekend as I needed the time to catch up on a personal project.

What's in your way?

In terms of coursework, nothing is in my way yet. I was able to install all the required tools on WSL, which I plan to use for my development environment for the rest of this class. I did not run into any issues so far following the workflow documentation to set up the Collatz project, and I am fairly confident in the rest of the work to be done. That said, I will definitely remain vigilant and give myself enough time to debug any future unintended issues that will inevitably occur.

However, my main obstacles are my suboptimal sleeping habits and my poor diet, both of which are solely my fault. I have not been able to get enough sleep before each lecture, and likewise, I do not have enough time to eat anything. As a result, I am usually exhausted throughout the rest of the day, which then usually causes me to lose my appetite. I tend to get nauseous when I am hungry, but I have a hard time eating because of that nausea. The combination of these factors can make it somewhat difficult for me to stay productive.

What will you do next week?

For the beginning of next week, I will have to prioritize the remaining work on the Collatz project, even if it means falling behind again on my personal work. Should the third Perusall paper be assigned, I also plan to start and complete my annotations earlier than usual in order to better distribute my future workload.

What was your experience of assertions, unit tests, pointers, and references?

I mostly understand assertions and their proper use, though until now I never had the thought to incorporate them into my programs. On the other hand, I am very accustomed to unit tests, pointers, and references. All of my prior extracurricular team projects required me to write unit tests for Continuous Integration, and I have always used both pointers and references for my code in C++.

How are you doing and holding up? What's been most helpful for you in terms of support at this time?

Besides those few physical and mental issues, I'm doing mostly alright. Of course, there is room for improvement in my habits, and at the end of the day I know I must hold myself accountable for that. In terms of support, regular naps have helped me fight off my exhaustion, and I am doing some research to see if there is an over-the-counter medication that could further support me through this class.

What made you happy this week?

I was able to spend more time than usual with my closest friends this week, which was only possible through an online voice call. We had all started playing a simple game together, and as silly as it may be, it made me feel included and genuinely happy to have the same shared interest with them.

What's your pick-of-the-week or tip-of-the-week?

When I was first starting C++ development, I remember spending a lot of time comprehending the intimidating double-pointer and reference-to-pointer syntaxes. Although I haven't used either case regularly since then, this week's lecture on pointers and references reminded me of their existence.

I figured it would be a good idea to revisit my understanding with a new perspective in this class, and this article does a good job of explaining the concept. In the end, it helped me the most to just think of the first pointer-asterisk as a fixed data type, which we can then choose to pass-by-reference or pass-by-pointer in the function parameters.
