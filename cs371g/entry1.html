Hello, I'm Kevin Liang, and this is my first blog entry for Summer 2020's CS371g: Generic Programming.

I've decided to use my personal webpage as a platform, which hopefully will help me gain more experience with frontend development.

Where did you grow up?

I spent the first fourteen years of my life in Utah, in the suburbs of the Salt Lake City area. After I finished middle school, my family moved to Scottsdale, Arizona. There, I attended Desert Mountain High School and graduated under the International Baccalaureate Diploma Programme.

What was your favorite extracurricular activity in high school?

In my senior year, a friend asked me to consider joining the school's First Robotics Competition (FRC) Team, #6314. The team's only student programmer from last season had quit, and they needed someone who could write and update the codebase for the new robot.

Although I was currently enrolled in the AP Computer Science course at the time, I had no prior programming experience. I was initially unsure if I was fit for the task, but I am glad I made the choice to join. My time on the team allowed me to learn, practice, and implement Java in a practical setting, and it was one of the major factors that sparked my interest in Computer Science.

Why did you come to UT Austin?

I actually had finished all my college applications before I realized that I wanted to pursue Computer Science. I was accepted into a few schools for my old choice of Aerospace Engineering, which included UT. When it was time to make a decision later, I knew that Computer Science was the right major for me.

However, the admissions process at UT Austin meant I was locked into Aerospace Engineering for my freshman year. I read that both programs at UT were both very competitive, which made the school seem like the safest choice in case I couldn't change my major. Fortunately, I was able to transfer into the Computer Science department here.

Why are you in this class?

For one reason or another, I wasn't able to secure an internship for this summer. I still wanted to remain productive, so I figured it would be a good idea to enroll in an online class and get further ahead with my degree plan.

This class is also my first Computer Science elective. To be quite honest, I don't really know how many upper-division courses I should be taking each semester to graduate on time. All I know is that I'll be taking Operating Systems in the upcoming fall semester, which I am sure will be an experience I won't easily forget for the rest of my life.

How much C++ do you already know?

I am relatively experienced with C++, as I have used the language over the past year with my software development position at the Texas Rocket Engineering Lab. Correspondingly, my knowledge of the language is mostly practical. I have written most of the code there to fulfill technical specifications and to interface with the ground mission control, hardware components, and central flight computers of a spacefaring rocket.

The only other external tool/library I have used is CppUTest, so I am excited to learn more about Boost, Google Test, and Valgrind. I feel it is optimal for any programmer to be familiar with a wide variety of tools. That way, he or she can make educated decisions when choosing one over another, and better adapt to the requirements of different companies and industry standards.

What's your pick-of-the-week?

I was going through my current development environment, and I just saw that WSL 2 had just released last month. WSL itself stands for Windows Subsystem for Linux, and essentially simulates a Linux environment on a Windows machine with very little overhead.

I have been using the previous, first version of WSL from the start. It is much more lightweight and quicker than dual-booting and any dedicated virtual machine, providing an exceptionally streamlined experience. WSL has allowed me to easily access and install tools such as gcc, g++, Valgrind, and more for use with my projects. And with the recent release of the WSL 2, not only is it faster but it can now support the full Linux kernel and full system call compatibility.

I definitely recommend WSL for any developer on a Windows device, and I certainly will continue to use it for this course.
