Entry #4

  • June 28, 2020
  • Kevin Liang
What did you do this past week?

This last week, I completed the Netflix assignment with my partner. We were able to reach an RMSE of about .9695 by using only the average of all the ratings of each individual and movie. We wanted to see if we could reach the extra credit goal of .95 by using another cache, but we ran out of time before we could fully implement that solution. I also began reviewing the notes for the earlier topics in the class and finished the summary for the assigned presentation on Ethics for Computer Science

What's in your way?

Nothing is in my way. I am very confident in all of the new material covered so far in the course, and I plan to keep up my review schedule to solidify my mastery when the exam comes on Wednesday.

What will you do next week?

As mentioned above, for the next week I will continue to study for the first test. I will probably go through the comprehensive list of topics and review every one of them in order to ensure nothing slips through. I will also look at code snippets for a better understanding of the proper syntax as well as the trickier cases of improper usage and undefined behavior.

What was your experience of RMSE, lambdas, function objects, Boost serialization, and iterators?

I understand both the concepts of RMSE and Boost serialization after using them both to complete the Netflix assignment. That said, I am still curious about the implementation and algorithm underneath Boost serialization, as my understanding mainly pertained to the use cases of compiling the libraries and reading the binaries. Serialization seems like an interesting topic as a whole, and it partially reminds me of the Huffman compression project from my Data Structures course.

Lambdas were fairly new to me, as I did not use them very much in Java either. I took some time to look at more examples outside of the transform function, which helped me understand their proper syntax and usage. Iterators were more familiar because I have used them in C++ before, though I am less experienced with the underlying concepts such as iterator_traits.

Finally, function objects were the most confusing to me, though only at first. After doing some more reading on that topic, they made much more sense to me. It was mainly seeing their usage and structure for the first time that caught me off guard, despite their simple requirement for implementation.

How are you doing and holding up? What's been most helpful for you in terms of support at this time?

I am doing better than usual. The upcoming exam has given me something to mentally focus my efforts on, which has been helpful during these times. As always, I am thankful for my friends for support, and I am fortunate to be able to continue talking with them through online measures.

What made you happy this week?

I redownloaded a virtual one-versus-one card game called Hearthstone after a two-year break. I was able to create a deck that is enjoyable for me, but absolutely not for my opponents. I have received some angry messages after winning games, which is completely understandable. The strategy relies on a completely unfair "combo" that can instantly win a game from any disadvantage, which makes me very happy every time it happens.

What's your pick-of-the-week or tip-of-the-week?

As I previously mentioned, I have been quite curious about the inner workings of Boost serialization. I tried looking for any further explanations for the algorithm, but I was actually not able to find anything. However, I did discover that Java has its own version of serialization for objects.

This article thoroughly explains the algorithm behind serialization used within Java, from the higher-level syntax to the lowest storage bytes of the serialized object. Overall, it was a very interesting read and presented an insightful perspective of practical data management and processing in the scope of programming.
